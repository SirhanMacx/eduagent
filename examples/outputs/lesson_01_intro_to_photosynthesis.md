# Lesson 1: What is Photosynthesis? The Big Picture

**Unit:** Life From Light: Understanding Photosynthesis
**Subject:** Science | **Grade:** 8 | **Lesson 1 of 15**
**Teacher:** Ms. Johnson

**Objective (SWBAT):** Students will be able to define photosynthesis, identify it as the process by which plants convert light energy into chemical energy (food), and explain why photosynthesis is essential to nearly all life on Earth.

**Standards:** MS-LS1-6, MS-LS2-3

**Total Time:** 50 minutes

---

## Do-Now / Warm-Up (5 min)

*Display on the board as students enter:*

> **Think About It:** You eat breakfast to get energy for the day. But plants don't eat — so where do they get their energy? Write your best guess in 2–3 sentences in your notebook.

After 3 minutes of silent writing, take 2–3 quick volunteer responses. Accept all answers without correcting — we'll come back to this at the end of class.

**Ms. Johnson says:** "I love hearing your ideas — some of you are already on the right track. Let's find out together."

---

## Direct Instruction (20 min)

### Part 1: What is Photosynthesis? (8 min)

**Ms. Johnson says:** "Alright, friends, today we're starting one of my favorite units in all of science. We're going to answer a question that sounds simple but is actually incredible: *How do plants make their own food?*"

**Key points to cover (project slides as you go):**

- **Definition:** Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make glucose (a sugar — their food) and oxygen.
- Write on the board: **Photo** (light) + **Synthesis** (to put together) = "putting together with light"
- Plants are **autotrophs** — they make their own food. Animals are **heterotrophs** — they eat other organisms for food.
- Ask: "So if plants make food from sunlight... what does that make the Sun?" (Answer: the ultimate energy source for most life on Earth.)

**Ms. Johnson says:** "I want you to really sit with this for a second. Almost every bite of food you've ever eaten — every sandwich, every apple, every cheeseburger — traces back to a plant that captured sunlight. That's photosynthesis at work."

### Part 2: Why Does It Matter? (7 min)

**Key points:**

- Photosynthesis produces **oxygen** — the gas we breathe. Before photosynthetic organisms evolved, Earth's atmosphere had almost no oxygen.
- Photosynthesis is the **base of nearly all food chains**. Plants → herbivores → carnivores. Remove photosynthesis and the whole chain collapses.
- Photosynthesis **removes CO₂** from the atmosphere — connection to climate (brief mention, we'll go deeper in Lesson 14).

Draw a simple chain on the board:
```
Sun → Plant (photosynthesis) → Rabbit (eats plant) → Fox (eats rabbit)
```

**Ms. Johnson says:** "See how it all starts with the Sun and a plant? That's the power of photosynthesis."

### Part 3: Autotrophs vs. Heterotrophs (5 min)

- Display a T-chart: **Autotrophs** | **Heterotrophs**
- Students copy into notebooks
- Fill in together with examples:
  - Autotrophs: oak tree, grass, algae, cyanobacteria
  - Heterotrophs: humans, dogs, mushrooms, fish

**Check for understanding:** "Thumbs up if an organism is an autotroph, thumbs down if it's a heterotroph." Call out: sunflower (up), eagle (down), seaweed (up), cow (down).

---

## Guided Practice (15 min)

### Leaf Observation Activity

**Materials:** One real leaf per student (or pair), hand lenses, printed observation sheet

**Instructions (read aloud):**

1. Hold your leaf up to the light. What do you notice? Record observations.
2. Look at the top surface vs. the bottom surface. How are they different?
3. Gently tear the leaf and look at the edge with your hand lens. Can you see any internal structure?
4. Sketch your leaf in the space provided. Label: top surface, bottom surface, veins, stem.

**Ms. Johnson says:** "I want you to really *look* at this leaf. This tiny thing is a solar-powered food factory. Everything we talked about today — photosynthesis — is happening right here."

**Circulate and ask probing questions:**
- "What do you think those veins are for?"
- "Why do you think the top of the leaf is darker green than the bottom?"
- "Where do you think the sunlight enters?"

After 10 minutes, have 2–3 students share their observations and sketches under the document camera.

---

## Independent Work (10 min)

### Graphic Organizer: "Photosynthesis — The Big Picture"

Students complete a provided graphic organizer with the following sections:

1. **Definition of photosynthesis** (in their own words)
2. **What plants need** (inputs — even if they're guessing at this point, that's okay)
3. **What plants make** (outputs)
4. **Autotroph vs. Heterotroph** (definition + 2 examples of each)
5. **Why photosynthesis matters** (at least 2 reasons)

**Ms. Johnson says:** "Use your notes, use your brain, use what we talked about. I don't expect perfection — I expect effort and thinking."

Collect at the end of class for a quick completion check.

---

## Exit Ticket (3 min)

*Hand out exit ticket slips as students finish their graphic organizers.*

1. **Define photosynthesis in one sentence.**
   *Expected: Photosynthesis is the process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make glucose (food) and oxygen.*

2. **Name one reason photosynthesis is important to life on Earth.**
   *Expected: It produces the oxygen we breathe / it is the base of food chains / it removes CO₂ from the air.*

3. **Is a mushroom an autotroph or a heterotroph? How do you know?**
   *Expected: Heterotroph — mushrooms don't photosynthesize; they get nutrients by decomposing other organisms.*

**Ms. Johnson says:** "Fill these out on your own — no talking, no peeking. This is just between you and me so I know how to help you tomorrow."

## Homework

**Read & Respond (15 min):** Read the one-page handout "Plants: Earth's Food Factories" and answer the 3 questions on the back. Due next class.

Questions:
1. What are the three things a plant needs for photosynthesis?
2. What two things does a plant produce during photosynthesis?
3. In your own words, explain why the author calls plants "Earth's food factories."

## Differentiation

### Struggling Learners
- Provide a **word bank** for the graphic organizer (photosynthesis, autotroph, heterotroph, glucose, oxygen, carbon dioxide, sunlight, water)
- Pair with a peer buddy during the leaf observation activity
- Allow the exit ticket to be completed verbally with the teacher if writing is a barrier
- Pre-highlight key terms in the homework reading

### Advanced Learners
- Challenge question on the graphic organizer: "Plants photosynthesize during the day. Do they also do cellular respiration? Research and explain."
- Ask them to predict what the *chemical equation* for photosynthesis might look like (we'll cover it in Lesson 7 — see if they can get close)
- Invite them to sketch a food web (not just a food chain) that starts with photosynthesis

### ELL Students
- Provide a bilingual glossary of key terms (photosynthesis, autotroph, heterotroph, glucose, oxygen, carbon dioxide)
- Use visuals and diagrams heavily during direct instruction — point to images as you speak
- Allow extra time on the exit ticket; accept drawings with labels as valid responses
- Pair with a bilingual peer if available during guided practice
